Best Sciatica Shots: What To Know

by August Otobong

When shooting leg pain lingers past rest, ice, and pills, injections can help tamp down the nerve irritation at the root of sciatica. The idea is simple: deliver anti-inflammatory medicine precisely where the nerve is pinched or inflamed, then buy time for healing and rehab. Relief can be meaningful—but it varies, and it’s not always permanent.

Why Doctors Use Injections

Sciatica stems from nerve irritation, often from a herniated disc or spinal stenosis compressing the sciatic nerve roots. When physical therapy, activity modification, and nonprescription pain relievers don’t move the needle after several weeks, doctors consider targeted shots to calm inflammation and reduce pressure. Guidelines outline when to consider injections: typically after 4–6 weeks of persistent, function-limiting symptoms, sooner if pain is severe or neurological deficits appear [1][2]. The most common approach is an epidural steroid injection, which bathes the irritated nerve root in corticosteroid to reduce swelling. Other options, like selective nerve root blocks or facet joint injections, can both diagnose and treat pain that mimics sciatica. The choice depends on the suspected driver—disc herniation, narrowing around the nerve, or facet joint irritation seen on exam and imaging.

Epidural Versus Nerve Blocks

Patients often ask how epidural injections compare with nerve blocks. Epidural steroid injections (ESIs) target the epidural space around the nerve roots and are the workhorse when a disc bulge or stenosis inflames the nerve. Nerve blocks place anesthetic (sometimes with steroid) directly at a specific nerve root or the medial branches that feed the facet joints. Facet injections help when back-dominant pain and imaging suggest arthritic joints rather than classic leg-dominant sciatica.

As for success rates, studies suggest many people experience short-term relief—often 50–70% report at least moderate improvement for weeks to a few months after an ESI, especially when a single herniated disc is the culprit [1][3][4]. Nerve root blocks may offer similar diagnostic and therapeutic benefit when symptoms map cleanly to one level. Facet injections help a subset with overlap pain. Your clinician weighs imaging, nerve distribution of pain, and exam findings to choose the best target.

What Relief Looks Like

A practical question is how long relief tends to last. For many, the benefit arrives within 2–7 days and can persist several weeks to a few months; some feel relief up to six months, though durability tends to fade with time [1][4]. Injections aim to create a window to progress strengthening, flexibility, and nerve gliding exercises that support longer-term improvement.

It’s not unusual for pain to feel worse in the first 24–48 hours after a cortisone shot—sometimes called a “steroid flare.” Ice, rest, and over-the-counter pain relief usually help; call your clinician for severe headache, fever, new weakness, or loss of bladder/bowel control. Recovery is typically straightforward: plan a ride home, take it easy the day of your procedure, avoid heavy lifting and strenuous workouts for 24 hours, and then resume therapy as advised. Some temporary leg numbness or heaviness can occur while anesthetic wears off.

Risks, Side Effects, Costs

As with any procedure, sciatica injections come with trade-offs. Potential benefits include targeted pain relief without systemic medication and a meaningful improvement in function that may delay or avoid surgery. Risks are uncommon but include bleeding, infection, dural puncture (spinal headache), transient leg weakness or numbness, allergic reaction, and—rarely—nerve injury or serious neurologic events [5][7]. Corticosteroids can cause short-term side effects like facial flushing, mood or sleep changes, and temporary blood sugar spikes. With repeated doses, steroid exposure may contribute to bone density loss, cataracts, or adrenal suppression; many clinicians limit ESIs to three to four per year, spaced out as needed [3][5].

Costs vary by region and facility. Facility plus professional fees can range from several hundred to over a thousand dollars. With insurance, out-of-pocket costs often depend on deductibles and co-pays, commonly ranging from $50 to several hundred dollars; without insurance, totals may run $500–$2,000 or more [8]. Ask your insurer and the procedure center for estimates beforehand.

Alternatives and Emerging Options

If you’re weighing options other than an epidural steroid injection, alternatives include structured physical therapy, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, neuropathic pain medications, and time—many disc herniations shrink over months. For persistent radicular pain, radiofrequency ablation can help if facet joints or medial branches are the pain generators, and surgery (like microdiscectomy) may offer faster relief when weakness, severe compression, or refractory pain persists [2][6]. Newer biologic approaches such as PRP (platelet-rich plasma) are being explored to support healing; while early data are intriguing, evidence remains limited compared with steroids, and coverage is inconsistent [6].

Bottom line: epidural steroids remain the most studied, commonly used shots for disc-related sciatica, often providing short- to medium-term relief by reducing inflammation and pressure on the nerve. Nerve blocks and facet injections target pain signals more directly when anatomy and symptoms point that way. Talk with your clinician about goals, the specific cause of your symptoms, and a plan that combines injections with rehabilitation—so any relief you gain translates into lasting function.
