New COPD Shots: What to Know

by August Otobong

For decades, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) care centered on inhalers. Now a new class of “shots” is changing the conversation. Biologic injections—most notably dupilumab (Dupixent)—target airway inflammation tied to flare-ups and are taken every few weeks. Here’s what patients keep asking about, from eligibility and benefits to costs and safety.

From Inhalers To Biologics

Standard COPD therapy relies on bronchodilators and inhaled corticosteroids to open airways and calm inflammation. Many people do well on dual or triple inhaler regimens, but some still endure frequent flare-ups, hospital stays, and a day-to-day breathlessness that narrows life’s options [5]. That’s driven interest in biologic add‑ons.

In 2024, the FDA cleared dupilumab as the first biologic injection for certain adults with uncontrolled COPD linked to “type 2” inflammation (often signaled by elevated blood eosinophils) [1]. Other advances, like newly approved nebulized ensifentrine (Ohtuvayre), show how fast the toolbox is evolving [4]. Biologics don’t replace inhalers; they’re layered on when exacerbations persist despite optimized inhaled care [5].

How These Injections Work

Here’s how monoclonal antibodies treat COPD: instead of simply widening airways, they home in on overactive immune pathways that drive mucus, swelling, and exacerbations. Dupilumab blocks the interleukin-4/13 receptor (IL‑4Rα), cooling a “type 2” inflammatory signature present in a meaningful subset of patients with COPD [1-3]. It’s given as a subcutaneous injection, typically every two weeks.

In the BOREAS and NOTUS trials, adding dupilumab to maximal inhaler therapy cut moderate-to-severe exacerbations by about 30% versus placebo and produced clinically meaningful gains in lung function and symptom scores [2,3]. The mechanism is targeted: these shots modulate upstream signaling that fuels flare‑ups rather than acting as rescue bronchodilators.

Other biologics target eosinophil pathways differently. Mepolizumab (an anti‑IL‑5 antibody) and benralizumab (anti‑IL‑5 receptor) reduce eosinophil activity. Trials in COPD showed mixed results—signals of benefit in specific eosinophilic subgroups for mepolizumab, but not consistent enough for FDA approval to date [6,7]. This research underscores the shift toward personalized treatment based on inflammation type.

Who May Be Eligible

Think of biologics as an option for people with severe or uncontrolled COPD despite standard inhalers. Typical flags include two or more moderate exacerbations (or one severe) in the past year, ongoing symptoms on dual/triple therapy, and evidence of type 2 inflammation—often a blood eosinophil count around 300 cells/µL or higher, though criteria can vary [1,2,5]. Talk with your clinician about whether your history, lab markers, and current regimen fit.

Because these therapies are targeted, clinicians may confirm adherence and inhaler technique first, then consider phenotyping (eosinophils, exacerbation pattern, comorbid asthma) and shared decision-making. Most patients receive injections every two weeks, with regular follow-up to monitor response, potential side effects, and the need for concomitant inhalers [1,5]. This is COPD care moving from one‑size‑fits‑all to a smarter match between biology and therapy.

Benefits, Risks, And Costs

What benefits should you expect? In the pivotal trials, patients on dupilumab reported fewer steroid bursts, fewer urgent visits, and improved lung function and health-related quality of life compared with placebo, on top of their inhalers [2,3]. Some people also describe steadier breathing and more “good days,” though real‑world results vary [5].

As for side effects, the most common with dupilumab are injection-site reactions, conjunctivitis, transient increases in blood eosinophils, and joint or muscle aches. Hypersensitivity reactions can occur; clinicians also consider helminth infection risk because IL‑4/13 pathways are part of parasitic defense [8]. Mepolizumab-type therapies have their own profiles—typically headache, back pain, and injection-site reactions—though they are not approved for COPD [6]. Always report new or worsening symptoms promptly.

Cost matters. Coverage for dupilumab is often handled under Medicare Part D (and may require prior authorization), though billing can differ if a clinic administers the dose. Out-of-pocket costs vary widely by plan design and subsidies, and manufacturer copay cards generally don’t apply to Medicare beneficiaries [9]. Ask your plan for a coverage determination and a detailed estimate before starting.

The long-term outlook is promising but still accumulating. Year‑long data show sustained reductions in exacerbations and meaningful symptom improvements in the right phenotype; ongoing studies will clarify durability, safety, and how best to integrate biologics with pulmonary rehab, vaccines, and smoking cessation [1-3,5].

What’s Next In Research

Beyond dupilumab, researchers are probing other inflammatory switches. Mepolizumab-type therapies showed modest, subgroup‑specific signals in METREX/METREO, fueling interest in better biomarker selection [6]. Benralizumab did not meet primary endpoints in GALATHEA/TERRANOVA, a reminder that COPD biology is heterogeneous and patient selection is crucial [7]. Agents targeting upstream cytokines such as thymic stromal lymphopoietin (TSLP) and the IL‑33 pathway are also under study for exacerbation‑prone phenotypes [5].

On the inhaled front, the approval of ensifentrine (Ohtuvayre) showed that innovation isn’t only injectable; combining bronchodilation and anti‑inflammatory effects via dual PDE3/4 inhibition adds another maintenance option for people across COPD severities [4]. Expect guidelines to keep evolving as health systems learn to identify candidates efficiently, monitor response, and pay for what truly works in the real world [5,9].

If you’re considering a biologic, bring this checklist to your next visit: exacerbation history, current inhalers and adherence, recent blood eosinophil count, vaccination status, smoking status, and questions about safety, monitoring, and insurance coverage. That conversation, anchored in your biology and goals, is where precision COPD care begins.
