What The New Dry AMD Shots Mean

by August Otobong

For years, Americans with dry age-related macular degeneration (AMD) had few options beyond vitamins and watchful monitoring. That’s changing. Newly approved eye injections for geographic atrophy (GA)—the advanced form of dry AMD—aim to slow the death of retinal cells. They won’t restore lost vision, but they can help preserve what remains [1].

Why Options Were Limited

Dry AMD progresses gradually as photoreceptors and supporting cells in the macula degenerate. Unlike “wet” AMD, which involves leaky blood vessels treatable with anti-VEGF drugs, dry AMD lacked a tractable drug target for decades. AREDS2 supplements can lower the risk of progression in intermediate disease, but they don’t stop GA once it begins. That scientific gap—how to interrupt the damage cascade—kept treatment off the table until recently [1,9].

How The Shots Work

The new shots modulate a branch of the immune system called the complement pathway, which can drive retinal cell injury when overactive. Pegcetacoplan (brand name Syfovre) blocks C3, an upstream complement protein; avacincaptad pegol (Izervay) targets C5, further downstream. By reducing this chronic inflammatory signaling, the injections are designed to slow GA growth. The goal is preservation, not reversal: stabilize the rate of atrophy, keep reading vision useful longer, and delay foveal involvement if possible [2,3].

Patients also ask how injections compare with AREDS2. They work differently and may be used together, depending on your stage. AREDS2 remains appropriate for many with intermediate AMD to reduce progression risk, while injections are intended specifically for eyes with GA confirmed by imaging and specialist evaluation [1,9].

Effectiveness And Trial Data

In large Phase 3 trials (OAKS and DERBY), Syfovre slowed GA enlargement by roughly a fifth with monthly dosing at two years, with every-other-month dosing showing slightly less effect. The benefit appeared to build over time, with greater separation from sham by month 24 [2,4].

Izervay’s GATHER2 trial showed a statistically significant reduction in GA growth at 12 months—about a mid-teens percentage versus sham—while earlier studies (GATHER1) suggested larger effects with longer follow-up [3,5]. As with complement inhibition generally, the effect is modest but meaningful when you’re trying to protect remaining central function, especially if the fovea is not yet involved. Practically, that can mean more time with usable reading, driving, or face-recognition vision before central vision declines—but it isn’t a promise of sharper sight [4,5].

Risks, Side Effects, Monitoring

Any intravitreal injection carries risks: eye irritation, floaters, a transient rise in eye pressure, and rare but serious infection (endophthalmitis). Postmarketing reports of retinal vasculitis—particularly following Syfovre’s launch—have drawn attention. These events appear uncommon but can be vision-threatening, which is why clinicians emphasize careful screening, informed consent, and updated safety guidance as real-world data accumulate [2,7].

In trials, both drugs were associated with higher rates of conversion to wet (neovascular) AMD than sham. The key is catching it early; wet AMD is typically treatable with standard anti-VEGF therapy if it develops [2,3]. Longer-term safety continues to be clarified through registries and post-marketing studies.

What to expect after a GA injection typically includes mild scratchiness, light sensitivity, and small floaters for a day or two. Severe pain, sudden vision loss, increasing redness, or discharge warrants an immediate call. Regular imaging (OCT and fundus autofluorescence) helps track atrophy size and check for fluid suggesting wet AMD. The tradeoff is straightforward: a modest slowdown in disease against ongoing injections, small but real risks, and diligent follow-up [2,8,10].

Eligibility, Access, And Practicalities

Who is a good candidate for geographic atrophy treatment? Generally, adults with confirmed GA from AMD on imaging, enough visual potential to benefit, and the ability to adhere to frequent visits. Those with fovea-sparing lesions may especially value preserved reading vision as atrophy growth slows. Your retina specialist will weigh lesion location and size, fellow-eye status, prior inflammation, and personal preferences. Eligibility depends on your specific disease stage and eye exam—it’s not one-size-fits-all [2,3,8].

The regimen requires commitment: injections monthly or every other month, often for the foreseeable future, plus monitoring for conversion to wet AMD. Ask about day-of logistics (driving, a companion if you prefer one, and appointment spacing).

Coverage matters too. In the U.S., physician-administered eye drugs are commonly billed under Medicare Part B when medically necessary, with typical cost-sharing (including coinsurance) depending on supplemental coverage; commercial plans may require prior authorization. Manufacturer support programs may help with copays and insurance navigation, but details vary—confirm specifics with your insurer and the clinic billing team before starting [2,3,6].

Finally, this isn’t always an either/or with vitamins. AREDS2 may still make sense for intermediate AMD or the fellow eye, while GA injections focus on slowing established atrophy. Lifestyle—don’t smoke, eat leafy greens and fish, manage blood pressure—still matters for retinal health alongside any high-tech treatment [1,9].
